Reports & Formal Writing

Level: B2 Writing
0 / 11 points

1. Report Structure & Purpose

A formal report presents factual information, analysis, and recommendations to a specific audience. Unlike essays, reports use headings, bullet points, and numbered sections for easy navigation.

SectionContent
Title / HeaderReport title, author, date, recipient
Executive SummaryBrief overview of findings (1 paragraph)
IntroductionPurpose and scope of the report
FindingsData, research, observations (objective)
AnalysisWhat the findings mean
RecommendationsAction steps based on the analysis
ConclusionBrief summary of key points

2. Formal vs Informal Register

Register is the level of formality in your writing. Reports require a formal register: impersonal, objective, and precise.

FeatureInformal ❌Formal ✅
ContractionsWe don't think…We do not consider…
First personI found that…It was found that…
Colloquial wordsThe plan was a big flop.The initiative proved unsuccessful.
Vague languageLots of customers complained.62% of respondents expressed dissatisfaction.
Emotive languageThe results are terrible.The results are below target.

3. Making Recommendations

Recommendations are a key part of any business report. They should be clear, specific, and actionable — and written using modal verbs to convey appropriate strength.

StrengthLanguageExample
Strong recommendationshould / must / it is essential thatThe company should review its pricing strategy immediately.
Moderate recommendationcould / may / it is advisable toManagement could consider offering flexible working hours.
Weak suggestionmight / it may be worthIt might be worth exploring alternative suppliers.
Tip: Recommendations in a report are typically numbered and written as bullet points, not as flowing prose. Keep each one concise and start with a verb: "Introduce…", "Review…", "Consider…"
