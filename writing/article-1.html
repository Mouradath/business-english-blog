Sentence Structure Basics

Level: A1/A2 Writing
0 / 11 points

1. What Is a Sentence?

A sentence is a group of words that expresses a complete thought. Every English sentence needs at minimum a subject (who or what) and a verb (the action or state).

Subject + Verb:
"Dogs bark." — Dogs = subject, bark = verb
"She reads." — She = subject, reads = verb

The Four Sentence Types

TypePurposeExample
DeclarativeStates a factShe works in London.
InterrogativeAsks a questionDoes she work in London?
ImperativeGives a commandWrite your name here.
ExclamatoryShows strong feelingWhat a great idea!

2. Subject, Verb & Object (SVO)

English uses a Subject–Verb–Object (SVO) pattern more consistently than many other languages. Understanding this pattern helps you write clearer sentences.

SVO Pattern:
"Tom [S] reads [V] books [O]."
"Maria [S] wrote [V] a letter [O]."
"They [S] are eating [V] lunch [O]."

Expanding the Pattern

PatternExample
S + VBirds sing.
S + V + OI love coffee.
S + V + complementShe is a teacher.
S + V + O + adverbHe drives his car carefully.

3. Simple, Compound & Complex Sentences

Good writers use a mix of sentence types to keep their writing interesting and clear.

Simple Sentences

One independent clause — one subject, one verb, one complete idea.

"The cat sat on the mat."

Compound Sentences

Two independent clauses joined by a coordinating conjunction (FANBOYS: For, And, Nor, But, Or, Yet, So).

"I studied hard, but the test was still difficult."

Complex Sentences

One independent clause + one dependent clause (starting with: because, although, when, if, since…).

"Although it was raining, we went for a walk."
TypeStructure
SimpleOne clause
CompoundClause + conjunction + clause
ComplexDependent clause + independent clause
Compound-ComplexMix of compound & complex
