How to Write Your First English Sentence:
Simple Rules That Always Work

Subject, verb, object — the golden formula behind every English sentence. Master this and you can write anything, from a simple message to a professional report.

📅 March 24, 2026✍️ English Language Blog📖 Writing · Article #01

Every building in the world — no matter how tall or complex — starts with a foundation. In English writing, that foundation is the sentence. And every English sentence — no matter how long or sophisticated — is built from the same simple core.

Once you understand this core, you will never stare at a blank page again. You will always know how to start, and how to build from there.

The Golden Formula: Subject + Verb + Object

Almost every English sentence follows this three-part pattern. Let's break it down simply:

The Golden English Sentence Formula
Part 1
Subject
+
Part 2
Verb
+
Part 3
Object
=
A Complete Sentence ✓

Subject — the person or thing doing the action (Who? What?)
Verb — the action itself (What are they doing?)
Object — the person or thing receiving the action (Who/what is it done to?)

Not every sentence needs all three — some sentences work with just a subject and verb. But this formula will never fail you.

See It in Action

Subject
Verb
Object
Sara writes emails.
The manager approved the report.
I love English.
Simple sentence
She reads books.
She = subject · reads = verb · books = object
Question sentence
Do you speak English?
Same formula, rearranged into a question.
Negative sentence
I do not have a pen.
Add "do not" or "does not" to make it negative.
Past sentence
He sent the document.
Change the verb to past form to shift time.

Common Mistakes Beginners Make

These are the most frequent sentence errors in beginner writing — and how to fix each one.

❌ Wrong
"She go to school every day."
✅ Correct
"She goes to school every day." (Add -s for he/she/it)
❌ Wrong
"I am go to work."
✅ Correct
"I am going to work." (am/is/are + verb-ing)
❌ Wrong
"He not like coffee."
✅ Correct
"He does not like coffee." (use does not / doesn't)
❌ Wrong
"Yesterday I go to the shop."
✅ Correct
"Yesterday I went to the shop." (use past tense)

From One Sentence to Many

Once you can write one sentence, you can write two. Then three. And before you know it, you are writing paragraphs. The secret is to connect your sentences with simple linking words like: and, but, because, so, then, also, however.

For example: "I woke up early. I had a meeting. I prepared my notes." becomes: "I woke up early because I had a meeting, so I prepared my notes beforehand." — far more natural and expressive.

Punctuation for Beginners:
Commas, Full Stops, and Question Marks

The basic punctuation marks that make your writing clear, professional, and easy to read — with the most common mistakes and how to fix them once and for all.

📅 March 31, 2026✍️ English Language Blog📖 Writing · Article #02

Punctuation is the traffic system of writing. Without it, your reader gets lost — they don't know when to stop, when to pause, or when something is a question. Good punctuation does not just follow rules — it guides your reader smoothly through your ideas.

In this article we cover the six most important punctuation marks for beginner writers, with clear rules and real examples.

💡

Good to know: Most punctuation mistakes come from just two things — missing full stops and overusing commas. Fix these two habits and your writing will immediately look more professional.

. Full Stop (Period)
Ends every complete sentence. The most important punctuation mark in English. Never skip it.
✅ "I sent the email." ❌ "I sent the email I will call later"
, Comma
Creates a pause. Used in lists, after introductory phrases, and to join two ideas.
✅ "I need pens, paper, and a notebook." ✅ "After the meeting, I sent the report."
? Question Mark
Ends every direct question. Replace the full stop with a question mark.
✅ "Are you available tomorrow?" ❌ "Are you available tomorrow."
! Exclamation Mark
Shows strong emotion or excitement. Use sparingly in professional writing.
✅ "Congratulations!" ⚠️ Avoid in formal emails — one per message maximum.
" " Quotation Marks
Used for direct speech or to highlight a specific word or phrase.
✅ She said, "I will be there by noon."
: Colon
Introduces a list, explanation, or example after a complete sentence.
✅ "Please bring three things: a pen, a notebook, and your ID."

Most Common Punctuation Mistakes — Fixed

❌ Wrong
"I went to the meeting it was very long I was tired"
✅ Correct
"I went to the meeting. It was very long and I was tired."
❌ Wrong
"We need, milk, eggs, and bread."
✅ Correct
"We need milk, eggs, and bread." (no comma after "need")
❌ Wrong
"Can you send me the report."
✅ Correct
"Can you send me the report?" (question needs ?)
❌ Wrong
"Dear Sara!!! I hope you are well!!!"
✅ Correct
"Dear Sara, I hope you are well." (one comma, no exclamations)

✍️ Punctuation Practice

Add the correct punctuation to each sentence below.

"I have three tasks to complete today write the report send the email and call the client"→ "I have three tasks to complete today: write the report, send the email, and call the client."
"Could you please confirm your attendance for Friday"→ "Could you please confirm your attendance for Friday?"
"After the presentation the team went for lunch it was a great afternoon"→ "After the presentation, the team went for lunch. It was a great afternoon."
🌿

Your challenge: Take something you wrote recently — a message, email, or note — and check every sentence ends with a full stop, question mark, or exclamation mark. Fix anything missing. This one habit will transform your writing immediately. ✍️

Linking Words: How to Connect Your Ideas Like a Native Writer

However, therefore, moreover, nevertheless — the linking words that transform separate sentences into flowing, coherent writing.

📅 April 7, 2026📖 Writing · Article #03

The difference between basic writing and sophisticated writing is often not vocabulary or grammar — it is how ideas are connected. Linking words show your reader the relationship between your thoughts: are you adding, contrasting, explaining, or concluding? Used well, they create a flow that is a pleasure to read.

FunctionLinking WordsExample
Adding informationFurthermore, Moreover, In addition, Also"The project is on schedule. Furthermore, costs are under budget."
ContrastingHowever, Nevertheless, On the other hand, Despite this"The plan is efficient. However, it requires significant investment."
Showing causeBecause, Since, As, Due to"Due to high demand, production has increased."
Showing resultTherefore, As a result, Consequently, Thus"Sales dropped. As a result, the team revised its strategy."
Giving examplesFor example, For instance, Such as, Namely"Several tools are available, such as email and video calls."
ConcludingIn conclusion, To summarise, Overall, In short"In conclusion, the project was a success."
SequencingFirst, Then, Subsequently, Finally"First, we gathered data. Then, we analysed it."
❌ No Linking Words — Choppy
"The team worked hard. The project was delayed. The client was unhappy. We revised our approach."
✅ With Linking Words — Flowing
"Despite the team's hard work, the project was delayed. As a result, the client was unhappy. Consequently, we revised our approach."

✍️ Linking Words Practice

Add the correct linking word to connect each pair of sentences.

"The report was complete. _____, the data was inaccurate." (contrast)→ "The report was complete. However, the data was inaccurate."
"We improved our process. _____, efficiency increased by 30%." (result)→ "We improved our process. As a result / Consequently, efficiency increased by 30%."
"The system has many advantages. _____, it reduces costs and saves time." (example)→ "The system has many advantages. For example, it reduces costs and saves time."

How to Structure a Paragraph That Actually Makes Sense

Topic sentence, supporting ideas, conclusion — the paragraph formula that never fails, with real before-and-after examples.

📅 April 14, 2026📖 Writing · Article #04

A paragraph is not just a group of sentences — it is a unit of one idea. Every strong paragraph contains one main thought, developed and supported clearly. Once you master this structure, your writing will always be organised and easy to follow.

The Perfect Paragraph Formula — PEEL
P
Point
+
E
Evidence
+
E
Explain
+
L
Link
=
Perfect Paragraph ✓
P — Point
State your main idea in the first sentence
The topic sentence tells the reader exactly what the paragraph is about. Everything else in the paragraph must support this one idea.
"Regular exercise has significant benefits for mental health."
E — Evidence
Support your point with a fact, example, or detail
Never make a claim without backing it up. Your evidence can be a statistic, an example, a quote, or a specific detail.
"Studies show that 30 minutes of exercise per day reduces symptoms of anxiety and depression by up to 47%."
E — Explain
Explain what the evidence means and why it matters
Connect the evidence back to your point. Don't leave the reader to figure out the connection themselves.
"This suggests that physical activity is not just good for the body — it has a profound and measurable impact on emotional wellbeing."
L — Link
Connect to the next idea or summarise
End by linking to the next paragraph or reinforcing your point. This keeps your writing flowing naturally.
"Given these benefits, incorporating exercise into a daily routine should be considered a priority, not an option."

✍️ Build a Paragraph

Use the PEEL structure to write a paragraph on the topic: "Learning English opens professional opportunities."

P — Write your topic sentence.→ "Learning English significantly expands professional opportunities in today's global workplace."
E — Add one piece of evidence or example.→ "English is the official business language in over 50 countries and is used in more than 70% of international meetings."
E — Explain why this matters.→ "This means that professionals with strong English skills have access to a far wider range of roles, companies, and markets."
L — Link or conclude.→ "Investing time in English is therefore not just a language skill — it is a career strategy."

Writing Emails vs Writing Messages: Knowing the Difference

Tone, length, and structure — how digital communication channels shape your writing style, and why mixing them up can send the wrong signal.

📅 April 21, 2026📖 Writing · Article #05

In the modern workplace, we communicate through emails, instant messages, chat platforms, and text messages — and each channel has its own unwritten rules. Using email language in a chat message looks over-formal. Using chat language in an email looks unprofessional. Understanding the difference is a key modern communication skill.

FeatureEmailInstant Message / Chat
LengthCan be longer — full paragraphsShort — 1–3 sentences ideally
Greeting"Dear / Hello [Name],""Hi" or no greeting needed
ToneFormal to semi-formalConversational and brief
Sign-off"Best regards / Kind regards"None, or "Thanks!" at most
PunctuationFull — punctuation mattersCasual — can skip full stops
Response timeHours or days acceptableQuick — usually minutes
EmojiAvoid in formal emailsAcceptable in casual work chats
❌ Email language in a chat
"Dear John, I hope this message finds you well. I am writing to enquire whether you received the document I sent earlier today. Best regards, Sara."
✅ Right tone for chat
"Hey John — did you get that doc I sent? Let me know if you need anything else 👍"

When to Choose Email Over Chat

Choose Email When...
The content is formal, important, or needs a record
Legal matters, client communication, formal requests, complaints, and anything requiring documentation should always be in email. You need the paper trail.
Choose Chat When...
It's a quick question or casual check-in
Quick yes/no questions, checking in with a colleague, sharing a link informally — these are perfect for chat. An email for these looks bureaucratic.

The Art of Concise Writing: Say More With Less

Cut the fluff, sharpen every sentence, and write with the clarity of a professional editor — because the most powerful writing uses the fewest words.

📅 April 28, 2026📖 Writing · Article #06

The greatest writers in history share one quality: they said exactly what they meant in as few words as possible. Concise writing is not cold or incomplete — it is respectful of the reader's time and confident in its message. Here is how to achieve it.

Cut These Wordy Phrases

❌ Wordy
"Due to the fact that"
✅ Concise
"Because"
❌ Wordy
"In the event that"
✅ Concise
"If"
❌ Wordy
"At this point in time"
✅ Concise
"Now"
❌ Wordy
"In order to"
✅ Concise
"To"
❌ Wordy
"The reason why is because"
✅ Concise
"Because"
❌ Wordy
"It is important to note that"
✅ Concise
Delete it — just state the point

The 4 Rules of Concise Writing

Rule 1
One idea per sentence
Long sentences with multiple clauses lose readers. If a sentence has more than two commas, split it.
Rule 2
Use strong verbs — not noun phrases
Weak: "We made a decision to..." → Strong: "We decided to..."
Weak: "She gave consideration to..." → Strong: "She considered..."
Rule 3
Delete filler openers
Remove: "It should be noted that..." / "It is worth mentioning..." → Just say the thing directly.
Rule 4
Read it aloud
If you run out of breath reading a sentence, it is too long. If you stumble on a phrase, it needs simplifying. Your ear is your best editor.

✍️ Concise Editing Practice

Rewrite each sentence to be more concise.

"Due to the fact that the deadline has been moved forward, we will need to make adjustments to our schedule."→ "Because the deadline has moved, we need to adjust our schedule."
"It is important to note that the results of the analysis clearly indicate that there is a need for improvement."→ "The analysis shows we need to improve."

Persuasive Writing: How to Make People Think, Feel, and Act

Structure, rhetoric, and language techniques used by the world's best communicators — so your writing moves people, not just informs them.

📅 May 5, 2026📖 Writing · Article #07

The most powerful writing does not just deliver information — it changes minds, moves emotions, and drives action. Persuasive writing is a skill used in proposals, cover letters, marketing, leadership communication, and everyday arguments. Here are the techniques that make it work.

The 3 Pillars of Persuasion (Aristotle's Framework)

Logos — Logic
Persuade with evidence, facts, and reasoning
Your argument must make logical sense. Use data, research, clear cause-and-effect reasoning. People must be able to follow your logic.
"Companies that invest in employee training see 24% higher profit margins on average — making this initiative not a cost, but an investment."
Ethos — Credibility
Persuade by establishing your authority and trustworthiness
Why should the reader believe you? Establish credibility through experience, expertise, balanced acknowledgement of counterarguments, and consistent, confident tone.
"Having managed cross-cultural teams for over a decade, I can say with confidence that this approach produces measurable results."
Pathos — Emotion
Persuade by connecting with values and feelings
Humans make decisions emotionally and justify them logically. Connect your argument to values your reader cares about — efficiency, fairness, safety, growth.
"This is not just a business decision — it is about the kind of company we want to be for the people who work here."

Power Phrases for Persuasive Writing

FunctionPersuasive Phrase
Acknowledging objections"While some may argue that X, the evidence clearly shows..."
Creating urgency"The time to act is now, not later..."
Building credibility"The data consistently demonstrates..."
Connecting to values"At its core, this is about..."
Closing with a call to action"I invite you to take one step: simply..."

✍️ Persuasion Practice

Logos (logic/data)→ "English is spoken by 1.5 billion people worldwide, and professionals with strong English skills earn up to 30% more on average."

Style Guide: Developing Your Own Voice in English Writing

How to write English that sounds distinctly like you — not a textbook, not a robot. Your voice is your greatest writing asset.

📅 May 12, 2026📖 Writing · Article #08

After grammar, vocabulary, structure, and clarity — comes the most advanced writing skill of all: voice. Voice is the quality that makes a piece of writing feel like it came from a real, specific human being. It is the tone, rhythm, and personality that makes your writing recognisably yours.

🎭
Voice is consistency of tone
Your writing voice is not about using unusual words — it is about maintaining a consistent attitude and rhythm across everything you write. Some writers are warm. Others are precise. Others are bold. The best know which they are — and lean into it.
📐
Voice is sentence rhythm
Short sentences create urgency. Long, flowing sentences create thoughtfulness. A mix creates dynamism. The best writers vary their sentence length deliberately, creating a rhythm the reader feels without noticing.
🌱
Voice develops through reading and imitation
Read writers you admire. Notice what they do. Try to imitate their rhythm in your own writing. Over time, your imitations blend into something uniquely your own. This is how all great writers develop.

Finding Your Voice — 5 Writing Exercises

Exercise 1
Write without editing for 5 minutes
Set a timer. Write continuously about anything without stopping to correct or improve. This unfiltered output reveals your natural voice most clearly.
Exercise 2
Rewrite a paragraph you admire in your own words
Take a paragraph from a book or article you love. Rewrite it completely in your own style. Notice which elements you naturally keep or change.
Exercise 3
Write the same event in three different tones
Describe a simple event — a meeting, a meal, a journey — in formal, casual, and humorous tones. Explore how far your voice can stretch.
Exercise 4
Read your writing aloud
If it sounds natural when spoken, your voice is authentic. If it sounds stiff or foreign to your own ear, simplify until it sounds like you talking.
Exercise 5
Keep a daily writing journal in English
Even 5 minutes per day. Write your thoughts, reactions, observations. Over months, you will see a voice emerging — confident, distinct, and genuinely yours.
