What Are Idioms? A Beginner's Guide
to Colourful English

Why "it's raining cats and dogs" has nothing to do with animals — and 10 more fascinating idioms that will make your English instantly more natural and expressive.

📅 March 24, 2026✍️ English Language Blog📖 Idioms & Culture · Article #01

Imagine you are having a conversation in English and someone says: "I think we should just bite the bullet." You look around. There is no bullet. Nobody is biting anything. Yet everyone in the room nods and moves on.

Welcome to the wonderful, puzzling, and colourful world of English idioms — expressions where the words together mean something completely different from their individual meanings. Once you understand them, you unlock a whole new layer of the English language.

💡

What is an idiom? An idiom is a fixed phrase whose meaning cannot be understood from the literal meaning of the individual words. English has over 25,000 idioms — but you only need to know a few dozen to sound impressively natural.

Literal vs Real Meaning — The Key to Understanding Idioms

The most important thing to understand about idioms is that you should never translate them word by word. Look at this example:

📖 Literal (word-by-word)
"It's raining cats and dogs" = Water is falling, and also cats and animals are somehow involved 🐱🐶❓
✅ Real Meaning
"It's raining cats and dogs" = It is raining very heavily. Nothing to do with animals at all! ☔

10 Essential Idioms Every Beginner Should Know

These are the most common idioms in everyday English conversation. Learn them, and you will start recognising them everywhere.

Idiom 01
"Break the ice"
Literally: Smash frozen water ❄️
Real Meaning
To do or say something to make people feel more comfortable in an awkward or unfamiliar social situation.
The teacher told a joke to break the ice on the first day of class.
He made a funny comment to break the ice at the meeting.
Origin: From the old practice of sending ships to break ice in frozen rivers to open trade routes — creating a path where there was none.
Idiom 02
"Under the weather"
Literally: Beneath the clouds ☁️
Real Meaning
To feel slightly ill or unwell. Not seriously sick — just not feeling your best.
I won't come to the office today — I'm feeling a bit under the weather.
She seemed under the weather at the party last night.
Origin: A nautical expression — sailors who felt seasick were sent below deck to shelter from the weather, literally going "under" it.
Idiom 03
"Hit the nail on the head"
Literally: Successfully strike a nail with a hammer 🔨
Real Meaning
To describe exactly the right answer, or to do something in exactly the right way. Used to praise someone's accuracy.
You hit the nail on the head — that is exactly the problem.
Her analysis really hit the nail on the head.
Idiom 04
"Bite the bullet"
Literally: Put a bullet in your mouth 😬
Real Meaning
To endure a painful or difficult situation with courage and determination. To do something you've been avoiding.
I have to bite the bullet and start studying for my exams.
She finally bit the bullet and asked for a raise.
Origin: In early surgery without anaesthesia, patients were given a bullet to bite on to distract from the pain — a vivid image of enduring discomfort.
Idiom 05
"Spill the beans"
Literally: Drop a container of beans 🫘
Real Meaning
To accidentally reveal a secret or surprise that was meant to be kept hidden.
Don't spill the beans about the surprise party!
He spilled the beans about the new product before the announcement.

10 Everyday Idioms You'll Hear in
Any English Conversation

From "break a leg" to "cost an arm and a leg" — 10 of the most common English idioms you will encounter every single day, fully decoded with real-world examples.

📅 March 31, 2026✍️ English Language Blog📖 Idioms & Culture · Article #02

Now that you know what idioms are, it is time to start building your collection. The idioms in this article are not rare or old-fashioned — these are expressions you will hear in films, at work, on podcasts, and in everyday conversation. Learn these ten and you will suddenly start recognising them everywhere.

💡

Learning tip: Don't try to memorise all ten at once. Read two or three today, use them in a sentence, then come back for more tomorrow. Slow and steady beats cramming every time.

Idiom 06
"Break a leg"
Literally: Fracture your leg bone 🦴
Real Meaning
Good luck! Used especially before a performance, presentation, interview, or exam. Saying "good luck" directly is actually considered bad luck in theatre culture — so "break a leg" became the alternative.
Your presentation is today? Break a leg!
She texted him "break a leg" before the job interview.
Idiom 07
"Cost an arm and a leg"
Literally: Pay with your body parts 💸
Real Meaning
To be extremely expensive. Used when something costs far more than expected or seems unreasonably priced.
That restaurant is beautiful but it costs an arm and a leg.
The repairs on my car cost an arm and a leg.
Idiom 08
"Once in a blue moon"
Literally: When the moon turns blue 🌕
Real Meaning
Very rarely — something that almost never happens. A "blue moon" is a rare astronomical event (a second full moon in one month), so the phrase means something equally rare.
I only go to the cinema once in a blue moon.
He calls his parents once in a blue moon — maybe twice a year.
Origin: Dates to the 1500s. An actual "blue moon" (second full moon in a month) happens roughly every 2–3 years — hence the rarity.
Idiom 09
"On the fence"
Literally: Sitting on top of a fence 🌿
Real Meaning
Unable to decide between two options. Neutral — not supporting either side. Uncommitted.
I'm still on the fence about whether to accept the job offer.
She seems on the fence about the new proposal.
Idiom 10
"The ball is in your court"
Literally: A sports ball is on your side of the court 🎾
Real Meaning
It is your turn to take action or make a decision. The responsibility has passed to you. Often used in business and negotiations.
I've sent the proposal — the ball is in their court now.
I've explained everything. The ball is in your court.
Idiom 11
"Burn the midnight oil"
Literally: Set fire to late-night lamp oil 🕯️
Real Meaning
To work very late into the night. To stay up late studying or working on something important.
I've been burning the midnight oil all week to finish this project.
She burnt the midnight oil before her final exams.
Origin: Before electricity, people worked by oil lamp at night. Burning oil late into the night meant working very hard after dark.

Business Idioms: The Phrases Every Professional Should Know

"Circle back", "on the same page", "touch base" — the idioms of the modern workplace decoded so you understand and use them with total confidence.

📅 April 7, 2026📖 Idioms & Culture · Article #03

Business English has its own unique vocabulary of idioms — expressions that fill every meeting, email, and office conversation. If you have ever heard "let's take this offline" and wondered what it means, this article is for you. These idioms are not just common — they are expected in professional settings.

Business Idiom 01
"Touch base"
Literally: Make physical contact with a base 🏃
Real Meaning
To briefly make contact with someone — a quick check-in or communication.
I'll touch base with you on Friday to check progress.
Let's touch base after the meeting.
Business Idiom 02
"On the same page"
Literally: Reading the same part of a book 📄
Real Meaning
To have the same understanding or agreement about something.
Before we proceed, I want to make sure we're all on the same page.
Are we on the same page regarding the deadline?
Business Idiom 03
"Take this offline"
Literally: Remove something from the internet 💻
Real Meaning
To continue a discussion privately or separately, not in the current meeting.
This is getting complex — let's take it offline and schedule a separate call.
Business Idiom 04
"Move the needle"
Literally: Change the position of a measuring needle 📊
Real Meaning
To make a noticeable or significant difference to a result or metric.
We need a strategy that will actually move the needle on sales.
This change isn't enough to move the needle.
Business Idiom 05
"Low-hanging fruit"
Literally: Fruit at the bottom of a tree, easy to reach 🍎
Real Meaning
The easiest opportunities — things that can be achieved with minimal effort.
Let's start with the low-hanging fruit before tackling the complex challenges.

Sarcasm, Humour & Irony in English: How to Get the Joke

English humour is layered, subtle, and often deadpan — here is how to finally understand it, and even use it yourself.

📅 April 21, 2026📖 Idioms & Culture · Article #05

English humour — especially British humour — can be deeply confusing for learners. Someone says something that sounds rude, but everyone laughs. Someone gives a compliment that is actually an insult. Understanding this subtle art of irony and sarcasm will not only help you understand native speakers — it will make you feel truly part of the culture.

The 3 Types of English Humour

1
Sarcasm — Saying the Opposite of What You Mean
Sarcasm is when words mean the exact opposite of their literal meaning. The tone of voice is everything. In text, it is harder to detect — look for context clues.
Someone arrives two hours late. Others say: "Oh, wonderful! So glad you could join us." (Meaning: We are NOT glad. You are very late.)

After a disaster: "Well, that went brilliantly." (Meaning: That went terribly.)
2
Understatement — Making Things Sound Smaller Than They Are
British English in particular loves understatement. Describing a catastrophe as "a bit unfortunate" or a broken leg as "slightly inconvenient" — this is classic British humour.
During a flood: "It's a bit wet outside."
After failing an important exam: "That could have gone better."
A major earthquake: "There was a minor disturbance."
3
Self-Deprecating Humour — Laughing at Yourself
English speakers — especially British ones — often make themselves the subject of a joke. This signals confidence and social intelligence. It is seen as charming, not weak.
"I organised this whole event — so naturally, everything went wrong."
"I have been studying English for years. You would never know."
😐 Taken Literally (Misunderstanding)
Someone says: "Oh, that's just brilliant." after a mistake. You think: "They liked it?" — but actually they are being sarcastic.
😄 Understood as Sarcasm
You notice the flat tone, the slight pause, the context of failure. You smile and say: "Yes, I know — back to the drawing board."

The Origin of 20 Famous English Idioms (And Why They Matter)

Understanding the history behind idioms makes them infinitely easier to remember — and far more interesting to use.

📅 April 28, 2026📖 Idioms & Culture · Article #06

Every idiom has a story. Understanding where an expression came from transforms it from a meaningless phrase into a memorable piece of history. Once you know the origin of an idiom, you will never forget its meaning.

Origin 01
"Kick the bucket"
Meaning
To die.
Origin: Most likely from the old English word "bucket" meaning a wooden beam or yoke. Pigs slaughtered in medieval times were hung from such a beam — their legs would kick the "bucket" as they died.
Origin 02
"Pull someone's leg"
Meaning
To joke with or tease someone.
Origin: Believed to come from 19th century street thieves who would literally pull victims' legs to trip them up before robbing them. Now used entirely humorously.
Origin 03
"Barking up the wrong tree"
Meaning
To pursue the wrong course of action based on a false assumption.
Origin: From 19th century American hunting — hunting dogs would sometimes track an animal to a tree, bark at it, but the animal had already escaped. The dog was barking at the wrong tree.
Origin 04
"Let the cat out of the bag"
Meaning
To accidentally reveal a secret.
Origin: At medieval markets, dishonest traders would sell a cat in a sealed bag, pretending it was a more valuable pig. If the bag opened, the cat (the secret) escaped.

Your investigation into the problem is "barking up the wrong tree." What does this mean for your approach?→ You are investigating the wrong thing / person. You need to reconsider your direction.

Cultural Taboos in English: What Not to Say and Why

Navigating sensitive topics, social norms, and the unspoken rules of English-speaking cultures — so you never accidentally offend.

📅 May 5, 2026📖 Idioms & Culture · Article #07

Every culture has unspoken rules about what not to say. In English-speaking cultures — particularly British, American, and Australian — certain topics, phrases, and questions are considered rude, invasive, or inappropriate. Understanding these unwritten rules protects you from accidental offence and builds cultural credibility.

💰
Never ask about salary or income directly
In most English-speaking cultures, asking "How much do you earn?" is considered deeply personal and rude — especially in professional settings. If salary comes up, it should be volunteered, never asked for directly.
⚖️
Avoid strong opinions on politics and religion
These topics are considered too divisive for casual conversation — especially with new acquaintances. In the UK, discussing politics with someone you don't know well is particularly frowned upon.
🎂
Commenting on age and weight
Pointing out that someone has gained weight, aged visibly, or looks tired is considered rude — even if meant kindly. In many other cultures this is normal; in English-speaking ones, it is not.
❓
Asking "Why aren't you married?" or "Why no children?"
Personal life choices about relationships and family are considered private. Asking about them — especially to adults you don't know well — can feel intrusive and uncomfortable.

What to Say Instead

Potentially RudeCulturally Safe Alternative
"How much do you earn?""What field do you work in?"
"You've put on weight!""You look well!" (if anything)
"Are you married? Why not?"Let the person share if they choose to
"How old are you?"Avoid unless in a very close relationship
🌿

Good to know: These are cultural norms, not universal rules. Different English-speaking countries vary. But when in doubt — especially in professional or new social settings — the safe approach is always to be warm, general, and let the other person lead the conversation deeper.

Idiom of the Month: One Expression, Deep Meaning

A recurring series — one fascinating idiom per month, explored in depth with origin, meaning, usage, and a challenge to use it yourself.

📅 May 12, 2026📖 Idioms & Culture · Article #08

The best way to truly own an idiom is to spend time with just one. Read its story, understand its layers, and then use it. This monthly series does exactly that — one idiom, examined from every angle.

Idiom of the Month — May
"The elephant in the room"
Literally: A large animal standing in an enclosed space 🐘
Real Meaning
An obvious problem or uncomfortable topic that everyone is aware of but nobody wants to discuss or acknowledge. The "elephant" is so large it cannot be ignored — yet people try.
No one mentioned the budget cut — it was truly the elephant in the room.
Let's address the elephant in the room: this project is behind schedule.
Origin: First used in 1814 by British poet Ivan Krylov, and later popularised in English. The metaphor is brilliant in its simplicity — the most obvious thing in a space is the one nobody names.
Deep Dive
Why this idiom is culturally significant
Cultural Context
English-speaking cultures — especially British culture — have a strong tendency towards indirect communication. Acknowledging "the elephant in the room" is therefore a courageous act. Saying these words signals that you are willing to have an honest, uncomfortable conversation when others are not. It is a phrase of bravery and directness.
In meetings: "I think there's an elephant in the room we should address."
In a relationship: "We've both been avoiding the elephant in the room."

Think of a situation in your life — at work, with friends, or in the news — where there is an "elephant in the room". Describe it using the idiom.

Example: "In our team meetings, the elephant in the room is..."→ ...the fact that our deadline is unrealistic and no one wants to say so to the manager.
