Figurative Language & Metaphor

Level: B2 Idioms & Culture
0 / 11 points

1. Types of Figurative Language

Figurative language goes beyond the literal meaning of words to create images, emotions, and deeper understanding. It appears everywhere — in literature, journalism, speeches, advertising, and everyday conversation.

DeviceDefinitionExample
MetaphorDescribes something as if it IS something else"Life is a journey."
SimileCompares using "like" or "as""She worked like a machine."
PersonificationGives human qualities to non-human things"The wind whispered through the trees."
HyperboleExtreme exaggeration for effect"I've told you a million times!"
EuphemismA mild word replacing a harsh one"He passed away" (= he died)
OxymoronTwo contradictory terms together"bittersweet", "deafening silence"

2. Conceptual Metaphors in English

Some metaphors are so deeply embedded in English that we don't notice them. These conceptual metaphors shape how we think and talk about abstract ideas.

ConceptMetaphorExamples in language
ArgumentArgument is WAR"He attacked every point." / "She defended her position." / "I demolished his argument."
TimeTime is MONEY"Don't waste my time." / "I've invested hours in this." / "Time is running out."
IdeasIdeas are FOOD"Let me digest that." / "I can't swallow that claim." / "That's food for thought."
LifeLife is a JOURNEY"She's at a crossroads." / "He's found his path." / "We've come a long way."

3. Similes in Everyday English

Similes are one of the easiest figurative devices to learn and use. Many are fixed expressions that native speakers use automatically.

SimileMeaning
as cool as a cucumbervery calm under pressure
as blind as a batunable to see well
as busy as a beeextremely busy and active
like two peas in a podvery similar in appearance or character
sleep like a logsleep very deeply
fit like a glovefit perfectly
In context: "The new CEO stayed as cool as a cucumber during the crisis — she slept like a log the night before the press conference, apparently."
