Cross-Cultural Communication

Level: B2 Business English
0 / 11 points

1. High-Context vs Low-Context Cultures

One of the most useful frameworks for understanding cultural differences in business is the high-context / low-context model. It explains why the same message can be interpreted very differently across cultures.

DimensionLow-Context (e.g. US, Germany, Scandinavia)High-Context (e.g. Japan, China, Arab countries)
Communication styleDirect, explicit, literalIndirect, implicit, relies on context
"No"Said clearly: "No, that won't work."Rarely said directly; shown by silence, vagueness
RelationshipsBusiness first, relationship secondRelationship first, business second
Decision-makingIndividual, fastGroup consensus, slower
ContractsLegally binding, detailedRelationship is the real contract

2. Navigating Cultural Differences

Awareness of cultural differences is the first step. The second is adapting your communication style without losing your professional identity.

Common Areas of Difference

AreaWhat varies
PunctualityArriving "on time" means different things in Germany vs Brazil
HierarchySome cultures expect formal deference to seniority; others are flat
DisagreementDirect challenge vs saving face — how you disagree matters enormously
SilenceIn some cultures, silence signals respect or thought; in others, discomfort
Gift-givingExpected in some cultures; seen as inappropriate in others
Tip: When in doubt, observe before acting. Ask culturally informed colleagues. And always err on the side of respectful formality — it's easier to relax than to recover from a perceived offence.

3. Language for Cultural Sensitivity

Even in English, how you phrase things across cultures matters. These phrases help you navigate sensitive moments diplomatically.

SituationCulturally sensitive phrase
Checking understanding"I want to make sure I've understood correctly — could you confirm…?"
Avoiding assumptions"I'm not sure how this is typically handled in your context — how would you approach it?"
Disagreeing tactfully"That's an interesting perspective. In our experience, we've found that…"
Showing respect for hierarchy"Would it be appropriate to discuss this with your senior team first?"
Acknowledging difference"I appreciate that our approaches may differ — I'm keen to find common ground."
