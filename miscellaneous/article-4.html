Confusing Word Pairs

Level: B1 Miscellaneous
0 / 11 points

1. Commonly Confused Verbs

Some English word pairs look or sound similar but have completely different meanings. Getting these wrong can cause serious misunderstandings.

Word AWord BDifference
lend (give temporarily)borrow (take temporarily)"Can you lend me a pen?" / "Can I borrow your pen?"
bring (towards speaker)take (away from speaker)"Bring me a coffee." / "Take this to her."
teach (give knowledge)learn (receive knowledge)"She teaches French." / "He learns fast."
rise (go up — no object)raise (lift something up)"Prices rise." / "They raised prices."
lie (be horizontal)lay (put something down)"She lies on the sofa." / "Lay the book on the table."

2. Confusing Adjectives & Nouns

Word AWord BDifference
affect (verb: to influence)effect (noun: the result)"Stress affects health." / "Stress has a bad effect."
principal (main / head of school)principle (a rule or belief)"The principal reason…" / "A moral principle."
complement (completes something)compliment (praise)"Wine complements food." / "She complimented my work."
fewer (countable nouns)less (uncountable nouns)"Fewer people, less time."
historic (important in history)historical (related to history)"A historic victory." / "A historical document."

3. False Friends & Near Misses

False friends are words that look like words in another language but have different meanings. Even within English, some pairs catch people out.

Classic English false friends for French speakers:
"Actually" ≠ actuellement → means "in fact / really"
"Eventually" ≠ éventuellement → means "in the end / finally"
"Sensible" ≠ sensible → means "reasonable / practical" (not "sensitive")
"Sympathetic" ≠ sympathique → means "understanding / compassionate" (not "nice")
WordCommon mistakeCorrect meaning
actuallyUsed as "currently"In fact / to be honest
eventuallyUsed as "possibly"In the end / after a long time
sensibleConfused with "sensitive"Reasonable, practical, logical
