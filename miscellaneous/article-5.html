1. Word Stress — The Key to Clear English

English is a stress-timed language — some syllables are emphasised (stressed) and others are reduced. Getting stress right is more important for clarity than perfecting individual sounds.

RuleExamples
Most 2-syllable nouns: stress 1st syllableTA-ble, WIN-dow, DOC-tor
Most 2-syllable verbs: stress 2nd syllablebe-GIN, de-CIDE, re-PEAT
Noun vs verb stress pairsRE-cord (n) / re-CORD (v)
Words ending in -tion, -sionstress syllable before: in-for-MA-tion
Compound nouns: stress 1st partHAND-bag, AIR-port, BOOK-shop
Stress shift pairs:
"I need to re-CORD this." (verb) / "Play the RE-cord again." (noun)
"There's a pro-TEST planned." (verb) / "They joined the PRO-test." (noun)

2. Silent Letters & Tricky Spellings

English spelling is famously irregular. Many words contain letters that are not pronounced — knowing these prevents common pronunciation errors.

Silent letterWords
Silent kknow, knife, knock, kneel, knight
Silent wwrite, wrong, wrap, wrist, whole
Silent bthumb, lamb, comb, debt, doubt
Silent hhour, honest, heir, vehicle
Silent ghnight, light, thought, daughter, through
Silent tlisten, fasten, castle, whistle

3. Connected Speech — How English Really Sounds

In natural speech, English words connect, blend, and change. Understanding this is key to both listening comprehension and sounding natural.

FeatureWhat happensExample
LinkingFinal consonant links to next vowel"an apple" → "a-NAPPLE"
ElisionSounds disappear"next day" → "nex' day"
AssimilationSounds change near each other"ten pence" → "tem pence"
Weak formsCommon words reduce in speech"to" → /tə/, "and" → /ən/, "of" → /əv/
ContractionsTwo words merge"I am" → "I'm", "do not" → "don't"
Natural speech example:
Written: "What do you want to eat?"
Natural: "Whaddya wanna eat?" — all features at work simultaneously.
