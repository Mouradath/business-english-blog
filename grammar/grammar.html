The 5 Tenses Every Beginner
Must Know

Past, present, future — made beautifully simple. No confusing rules, just five essential tenses with real examples you can start using today.

📅 March 24, 2026✍️ English Language Blog📖 Grammar · Article #01

If you are just starting to learn English, tenses can feel overwhelming. There are technically twelve tenses — but here is a liberating secret: you only need five to communicate confidently in everyday life.

Think of tenses as time labels. They tell the person listening when something happens — in the past, right now, or in the future. Master these five and you can talk about almost anything.

💡

Tip: Don't memorise all five at once. Read each one, look at the examples, then try making one sentence of your own. That is the fastest way to make them stick.

Tense 01 of 05
Simple Present
What is always true or happens regularly
Use the Simple Present for things that are always true, things you do regularly or as a habit, and general facts. It is the most common tense in everyday conversation.
Formula
Subject + verb (+ s/es for he/she/it)
Example
I work every day.
Example
She speaks English.
Example
The sun rises in the east.
Example
We eat lunch at noon.
Use it when: Talking about routines, habits, facts, and things that are always true.
Tense 02 of 05
Simple Past
What already happened — and is finished
Use the Simple Past for actions that started and finished in the past. It is very common in storytelling. Most verbs just add -ed — but watch out for irregular verbs like go → went, write → wrote!
Formula
Subject + verb-ed (or irregular past form)
Example
I worked yesterday.
Example
She went to school.
Example
They watched a film.
Example
He wrote an email.
Use it when: Talking about completed actions, past events, or telling a story.
Tense 03 of 05
Simple Future
What will happen next
Use the Simple Future for things that have not happened yet. The most common way is simply adding "will" before the verb. Easy — it works for every subject without any changes.
Formula
Subject + will + verb (base form)
Example
I will call you tomorrow.
Example
She will study tonight.
Example
It will rain this evening.
Example
We will meet on Monday.
Use it when: Making predictions, promises, spontaneous decisions, or future plans.
Tense 04 of 05
Present Continuous
What is happening right now, at this moment
Use the Present Continuous for actions that are happening right now as you speak, or temporary situations currently in progress. The key ingredient is adding "-ing" to the verb.
Formula
Subject + am/is/are + verb-ing
Example
I am reading right now.
Example
She is cooking dinner.
Example
They are learning English.
Example
It is raining outside.
Use it when: Describing what is happening at this very moment or a temporary ongoing action.
Tense 05 of 05
Present Perfect
What happened in the past — but connects to now
The Present Perfect bridges past and present. Use it when a past action has a result or connection in the present. The exact time is usually not important. Key words: already, just, ever, never, yet.
Formula
Subject + have/has + past participle
Example
I have finished my work.
Example
She has visited London.
Example
I have never eaten sushi.
Example
Have you ever tried this?
Use it when: Talking about life experiences, recent actions, or results that still matter now.

Quick Reference: All 5 at a Glance

TenseWhen to UseSignal Words
Simple PresentHabits, facts, routinesevery day, always, usually
Simple PastFinished past actionsyesterday, last week, ago
Simple FutureFuture plans & predictionstomorrow, next week, will
Present ContinuousActions happening nowright now, currently, at the moment
Present PerfectPast with present resultalready, just, ever, never, yet

✍️ Quick Practice

Complete each sentence using the correct tense shown. Answers revealed below each one.

"Every morning, I _____ (drink) coffee." → Simple Present→ Every morning, I drink coffee.
"Yesterday, she _____ (send) an email." → Simple Past→ Yesterday, she sent an email.
"Tomorrow, we _____ (have) a meeting." → Simple Future→ Tomorrow, we will have a meeting.
"Right now, he _____ (read) a book." → Present Continuous→ Right now, he is reading a book.
"I _____ (finish) my homework already." → Present Perfect→ I have finished my homework already.

Articles: When to Use "A", "An", and "The"
(And When to Use Nothing)

One of the trickiest parts of English — explained with simple, memorable rules and dozens of real examples you can apply immediately.

📅 March 31, 2026✍️ English Language Blog📖 Grammar · Article #02

If your native language does not have articles, English articles can feel completely mysterious. Why do we say "a dog" but "the dog"? Why "an apple" but "a university"? And sometimes — no article at all?

The good news: there are clear rules. And once you understand the logic behind them, articles will start to feel completely natural.

💡

English has only three articles: a, an, and the. That is it. Learning when to use each one — and when to use none — is the entire challenge.

Article 01
"A" — The Indefinite Article
One of many — not specific
Use "a" when you are talking about one of many things — not a specific one. The listener does not know which one yet. Also use "a" before words that start with a consonant sound.
Rule
Use "a" + singular noun starting with consonant sound
✅ Correct
I saw a dog in the park.
✅ Correct
She works at a bank.
✅ Correct
He bought a car.
✅ Correct
I need a pen.
Remember: "a university" not "an university" — because "university" starts with a Y sound (consonant), not a vowel sound.
Article 02
"An" — Also Indefinite, But for Vowel Sounds
Same as "a" — but before vowel sounds
Use "an" before words that begin with a vowel sound (a, e, i, o, u sounds). This is purely about the sound, not the spelling. "An hour" — because "hour" starts with an "ow" sound.
Rule
Use "an" + singular noun starting with vowel sound
✅ Correct
I ate an apple.
✅ Correct
She is an engineer.
✅ Correct
It took an hour.
✅ Correct
He made an error.
Tricky ones: "an honest person" (h is silent), "a European country" (starts with Y sound), "an MBA" (starts with "em" sound).
Article 03
"The" — The Definite Article
This specific one — both speaker and listener know which
Use "the" when you are talking about a specific thing that both the speaker and listener know about. It could be mentioned before, unique in context, or the only one in existence.
Rule
Use "the" when both parties know exactly which one
✅ Correct
Close the door. (we both know which door)
✅ Correct
The sun is bright. (only one sun)
✅ Correct
I loved the film. (mentioned before)
✅ Correct
The President spoke today.
Key insight: First mention = "a/an". Second mention = "the". "I saw a dog. The dog was huge."
Article 04
No Article (Zero Article)
General statements, proper nouns, and uncountable nouns
Sometimes English uses no article at all. This happens with plural general statements, uncountable nouns used generally, most proper nouns (names, countries, languages), and abstract concepts.
Rule
No article for general plurals, languages, countries, and uncountable general nouns
✅ Correct
Dogs are friendly. (all dogs in general)
✅ Correct
She speaks English.
✅ Correct
I love music.
✅ Correct
He lives in France.
Compare: "I love music" (general) vs "I love the music at that restaurant" (specific).

✍️ Article Practice

Choose the correct article (a / an / the / nothing) for each sentence.

"She is ___ engineer who works at ___ big company."→ She is an engineer who works at a big company.
"I bought ___ new phone. ___ phone is really fast."→ I bought a new phone. The phone is really fast.
"___ sun rises in ___ east."→ The sun rises in the east.
"She speaks ___ French and ___ little English."→ She speaks (no article) French and a little English.
🌿

Your challenge: Read any short English text today and circle every article you see. Ask yourself: why was "a", "an", or "the" used there? This simple habit will make articles feel intuitive very quickly.

Questions in English: How to Ask Anything Correctly

Yes/no questions, Wh- questions, and indirect questions — all the question structures you need, explained simply with real everyday examples.

📅 April 7, 2026📖 Grammar · Article #03

Questions are the engine of conversation — they keep it moving, show interest, and open doors. English has three main types of questions, each with its own structure. Master all three and you can ask anything you need to in any situation.

Question Type 01
Yes/No Questions
Questions that expect "yes" or "no" as the answer
These are formed by putting the auxiliary verb (do, does, did, is, are, will, can, have) before the subject. The main verb stays in its base or original form.
Formula
Auxiliary verb + Subject + Main verb?
Example
Do you speak English?
Example
Is she available?
Example
Did he call you?
Example
Can I help you?
Remember: Never say "You speak English?" as a real question — always invert the verb and subject.
Question Type 02
Wh- Questions
Open questions asking for specific information
These begin with a question word: What, Where, When, Why, Who, How, Which, Whose. They follow the yes/no structure but with the question word added at the front.
Formula
Question word + Auxiliary + Subject + Verb?
Example
What do you do?
Example
Where does she work?
Example
When did they arrive?
Example
How can I help?
Question Type 03
Indirect Questions
Polite, embedded questions — very useful in professional settings
These are questions hidden inside a polite phrase. They use normal word order (not inverted) and sound much more formal and respectful. Extremely useful in business and formal situations.
Formula
Polite opener + question word + Subject + Verb
Direct → Indirect
"Where is it?" → "Could you tell me where it is?"
Direct → Indirect
"What time does it start?" → "Do you know what time it starts?"
Polite openers: "Could you tell me...", "Do you know...", "I was wondering if...", "Would you mind telling me..."

✍️ Question Practice

Convert each statement into the question type specified.

"She works here." → Make a yes/no question.→ Does she work here?
"The meeting starts at 10." → Make a Wh- question asking about time.→ When does the meeting start?
"Where is the office?" → Make this an indirect question.→ Could you tell me where the office is?

Conditionals Explained: If This, Then That — Simply

First, second, and third conditionals — with real-life scenarios and everyday examples that finally make these essential structures click.

📅 April 14, 2026📖 Grammar · Article #04

Conditionals let you talk about possibilities, hypothetical situations, and regrets. They are one of the most expressive structures in English — and once you understand the logic, they are much simpler than they appear.

Conditional 01
First Conditional — Real & Likely
Real possibilities in the present or future
Use when the situation is possible and likely to happen. You are talking about a real possible future result.
Formula
If + Present Simple → will + base verb
Example
If I study, I will pass.
Example
If it rains, we will stay home.
Example
If she calls, I will answer.
Example
If you ask, they will help.
Real life use: "If I finish early, I will send you the report today."
Conditional 02
Second Conditional — Imaginary Present
Hypothetical or unlikely situations right now
Use when the situation is imaginary, unlikely, or impossible in the present. You are describing a fantasy or hypothesis.
Formula
If + Past Simple → would + base verb
Example
If I had more time, I would learn Spanish.
Example
If she were here, she would help.
Example
If I won the lottery, I would travel.
Example
If you asked him, he would say yes.
Note: Use "were" instead of "was" for all subjects in formal writing: "If I were you, I would reconsider."
Conditional 03
Third Conditional — Past Regrets
Imagining a different past — things that cannot be changed
Use when you are talking about a past situation that did not happen and imagining a different result. Often used to express regret.
Formula
If + Past Perfect → would have + past participle
Example
If I had studied, I would have passed.
Example
If she had called, I would have come.
Real life use: "If I had left earlier, I would not have missed the train." (But I didn't leave early — this is a regret.)

✍️ Conditional Practice

Complete each sentence using the correct conditional.

"If I _____ (have) more money, I _____ (buy) a new laptop." Which conditional? Imaginary present.→ If I had more money, I would buy a new laptop. (2nd conditional)
"If she _____ (study) harder, she _____ (pass) the exam." Which conditional? Real future possibility.→ If she studies harder, she will pass the exam. (1st conditional)
"If he _____ (leave) on time, he _____ (miss, negative) the flight." Past — it already happened.→ If he had left on time, he would not have missed the flight. (3rd conditional)

Active vs Passive Voice: Which One Should You Use and When?

Understanding the difference between active and passive voice — and using both confidently in writing and professional communication.

📅 April 21, 2026📖 Grammar · Article #05

Voice is one of the most powerful writing tools in English. The same information can be communicated in two fundamentally different ways — and the choice between them changes the entire feel of your sentence. Knowing when to use each one is a mark of a sophisticated English writer.

Voice 01
Active Voice
The subject does the action — direct, clear, and energetic
In active voice, the subject performs the action. This is the default in English and is almost always clearer and more direct. Use it for most of your writing.
Formula
Subject + Verb + Object
Active
The manager approved the report.
Active
Sara wrote the email.
Active
The team completed the project.
Active
I made an error.
Voice 02
Passive Voice
The subject receives the action — formal, distant, impersonal
In passive voice, the subject receives the action. The performer is either unknown, unimportant, or deliberately omitted. Common in formal, scientific, and official writing.
Formula
Subject + be (was/is/will be) + past participle (+ by...)
Passive
The report was approved.
Passive
The email was written by Sara.
Passive
The project has been completed.
Passive
An error was made.

When to Use Each Voice

Use Active When...Use Passive When...
You want to be clear and directThe doer is unknown or unimportant
Writing everyday communicationWriting formal reports or scientific documents
You want to show who is responsibleYou want to avoid blaming someone directly
You want energy and movement in writingYou want a neutral, impersonal tone

✍️ Active ↔ Passive Practice

Convert each sentence as directed.

"The committee reviewed the proposal." → Make passive.→ "The proposal was reviewed by the committee."
"The report was submitted by the team." → Make active.→ "The team submitted the report."
Write one sentence about your work in active voice, then rewrite it in passive.→ Active: "I completed the analysis." Passive: "The analysis was completed."

Modal Verbs: Could, Would, Should, Must — Mastered at Last

The complete guide to modal verbs — with examples from emails, conversations, and professional writing so they finally make perfect sense.

📅 April 28, 2026📖 Grammar · Article #06

Modal verbs are small but incredibly powerful. They add layers of meaning — possibility, permission, obligation, advice, and politeness — to any sentence. The good news: modal verbs never change form and always follow the same rule: modal + base verb.

ModalMain UsesExample
CanAbility / Informal permission"I can speak English." / "Can I help?"
CouldPast ability / Polite request / Possibility"Could you send this?" / "It could be late."
WillFuture / Strong intention"I will send it tomorrow."
WouldPolite request / Conditional / Preference"Would you mind?" / "I would like to..."
ShouldAdvice / Recommendation / Expectation"You should try this." / "It should work."
MustStrong obligation / Logical deduction"You must submit by Friday." / "She must be tired."
MayFormal permission / Possibility"May I ask a question?" / "It may rain."
MightWeaker possibility"I might be late." / "That might work."

Modals in Professional English

Modal verbs are the backbone of polite professional language. Notice how they change the tone dramatically:

Direct (no modal)Polite (with modal)
"Send me the file.""Could you send me the file?"
"I want a meeting.""I would like to arrange a meeting."
"Do this by Friday.""This should be completed by Friday."
"You are wrong.""That might not be entirely accurate."

✍️ Modal Practice

Fill in the correct modal verb.

"_____ you please review this document?" (polite request)→ Could you please review this document?
"You _____ submit the form before the deadline." (obligation)→ You must submit the form before the deadline.
"I _____ be available on Thursday — let me check." (possibility)→ I might be available on Thursday.
"_____ I ask you a question?" (formal permission)→ May I ask you a question?

Subjunctive Mood: The Grammar Feature Most People Forget

Rarely taught but widely used — understand and use the subjunctive mood naturally in formal writing and professional communication.

📅 May 5, 2026📖 Grammar · Article #07

The subjunctive mood is one of English's most overlooked grammar features — yet it appears constantly in formal writing, business communication, and literary English. Once you recognise it, you will see it everywhere. And once you use it, your writing will sound remarkably sophisticated.

💡

The subjunctive is used to express wishes, recommendations, hypothetical situations, and formal suggestions. It uses the base form of the verb (no -s, no was — always "be" and "were").

Subjunctive Use 01
After "suggest", "recommend", "request", "insist"
Formal recommendations and demands
When these verbs appear in formal writing, the verb in the following clause stays in its base form regardless of the subject.
Formula
suggest/recommend/request/insist + that + subject + base verb
Example
I suggest that he be informed immediately.
Example
We recommend that she attend the meeting.
Example
The report insists that action be taken.
Example
I request that this remain confidential.
Subjunctive Use 02
"If I were..." — Hypothetical Situations
Always use "were" not "was" in formal hypotheticals
In formal and written English, always use "were" for all subjects in hypothetical "if" clauses — even "I", "he", "she".
Formula
If + subject + were (not was) + rest of sentence
Formal
If I were in your position...
Formal
If she were to accept...
Formal
Were this to happen...
Formal
If he were available...
Subjunctive Use 03
Fixed Phrases and Formal Expressions
Subjunctive locked into common formal expressions
Some expressions in English have the subjunctive frozen inside them. These appear frequently in writing and formal speech.
Expression
Be that as it may (even so)
Expression
As it were (so to speak)
Expression
God save the King
Expression
Come what may (whatever happens)

✍️ Subjunctive Practice

Correct the error in each sentence.

"I suggest that he explains the situation." → Fix the verb.→ "I suggest that he explain the situation." (base form, no -s)
"If I was you, I would reconsider." → Fix for formal writing.→ "If I were you, I would reconsider." (were, not was)

Common Grammar Mistakes Even Advanced Speakers Make

Fine-tuning your grammar at the highest level — the persistent errors that slip through even when you think your English is perfect.

📅 May 12, 2026📖 Grammar · Article #08

Advanced English speakers often have one blind spot: the errors they have been making for years feel correct — because they have always made them. This article targets the most common persistent mistakes that appear even at high levels, so you can finally eliminate them for good.

❌ Wrong
"Between you and I, this was a mistake."
✅ Correct
"Between you and me..." (use object pronoun after prepositions)
❌ Wrong
"The data shows that..."
✅ Correct
"The data show that..." (data is technically plural)
❌ Wrong
"I wish I would have known earlier."
✅ Correct
"I wish I had known earlier." (wish + past perfect)
❌ Wrong
"Less people attended than expected."
✅ Correct
"Fewer people attended." (fewer = countable, less = uncountable)
❌ Wrong
"I am very agree with you."
✅ Correct
"I strongly agree with you." (agree is a verb, not an adjective)
❌ Wrong
"She is comprised of three departments."
✅ Correct
"She comprises three departments." or "is composed of"
