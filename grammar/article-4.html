The Present Perfect Explained

One of the trickiest tenses in English — finally made simple and clear.

← Back to Grammar

Why Is the Present Perfect So Confusing?

The Present Perfect connects the past to the present. It talks about past actions that have a connection or relevance to now. Many learners use the Past Simple when they should use the Present Perfect — let's fix that!

Past SimplePresent Perfect
I ate lunch at 1pm.I have eaten lunch.
She visited Paris in 2019.She has visited Paris.
He lost his keys yesterday.He has lost his keys.

How to Form the Present Perfect

Subject + have/has + past participle

I/You/We/They + have + worked → I have worked

He/She/It + has + worked → She has worked

Negative: I haven't worked / She hasn't worked

Question: Have you worked? / Has she worked?

Four Main Uses

1. Life Experiences

To talk about experiences in your life — the exact time is not important.

I have visited Japan. (sometime in my life)

She has never eaten sushi.

Have you ever seen the Northern Lights?

2. Recent Actions with Present Results

A past action that has a clear result or effect right now.

I have lost my keys. (= I can't find them now)

She has broken her leg. (= her leg is broken now)

He has left the office. (= he is not here now)

3. Unfinished Time Periods

Actions in a time period that has not finished yet — today, this week, this year.

I have drunk three coffees today.

She has worked hard this week.

We have not had any rain this month.

4. With "Just", "Already" and "Yet"

Just = very recently: I have just finished my homework.

Already = sooner than expected: She has already left.

Yet = expected but not happened: I haven't eaten yet. / Have you finished yet?

🕐 Signal Words for Present Perfect

ever · never · already · yet · just · recently · lately · since · for · so far · this week/month/year

Present Perfect vs Past Simple

Use Past Simple when you mention a specific finished time. Use Present Perfect when the time is not mentioned or not finished.

❌ Wrong

I have seen that film last Tuesday.

Did you ever visit Rome?

✅ Correct

I saw that film last Tuesday. (specific time → Past Simple)

Have you ever visited Rome? (life experience → Present Perfect)

✅ What You Learned

Present Perfect = have/has + past participle. Use it for life experiences, recent results, unfinished time, and with just/already/yet. If the time is specific and finished, use Past Simple instead.
