Conditionals: Zero, First and Second

Express possibilities and hypothetical situations with confidence.

← Back to Grammar

What Are Conditionals?

Conditionals are sentences with if. They describe what happens — or might happen — under certain conditions. There are four main types. This article covers the three most essential ones.

TypeUseExample
ZeroAlways true factsIf you heat water, it boils.
FirstReal future possibilityIf it rains, I will stay home.
SecondUnreal/hypotheticalIf I had money, I would travel.

Zero Conditional

Used for facts that are always true — scientific facts, general truths, and habits.

Formula: If + Present Simple, Present Simple

If you heat ice, it melts.

If it rains, the ground gets wet.

If I drink coffee at night, I can't sleep.

💡 Tip

You can replace "if" with "when" in zero conditionals: "When you heat ice, it melts."

First Conditional

Used for real and possible situations in the future.

Formula: If + Present Simple, will + verb

If it rains tomorrow, I will stay home.

If she studies hard, she will pass the exam.

If you don't hurry, we will miss the bus.

💡 Tip

Never use "will" in the if-clause: ❌ "If it will rain..." ✅ "If it rains..."

Second Conditional

Used for unreal, imaginary, or unlikely situations in the present or future.

Formula: If + Past Simple, would + verb

If I had more money, I would travel the world.

If she were taller, she would be a model.

If I spoke Chinese, I would move to Shanghai.

💡 Note on "Were"

In formal English, always use "were" for all subjects in second conditionals: "If I were you..." / "If she were here..." — not "was".

Common Mistakes

❌ Wrong

If I will see him, I will tell him.

If I would have money, I would buy a car.

✅ Correct

If I see him, I will tell him. (First conditional)

If I had money, I would buy a car. (Second conditional)

✅ What You Learned

Zero = always true facts. First = real future possibilities. Second = imaginary situations. Never use "will" or "would" in the if-clause!
