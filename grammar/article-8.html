Nuances of English Modal Verbs

Explore the subtle differences between modal verbs that even advanced learners miss.

← Back to Grammar

Beyond the Basics

You already know that "can" means ability and "must" means obligation. But modal verbs carry much deeper meanings depending on context, tone, and situation. This article explores those nuances.

Degrees of Certainty

Modal verbs express how certain or likely something is.

ModalCertaintyExample
mustAlmost certain (deduction)She must be tired — she worked all day.
shouldFairly likely (expectation)The parcel should arrive tomorrow.
mayPossible (neutral)It may rain later.
mightLess certainI might come to the party.
can't/couldn'tAlmost impossibleThat can't be right!

Must vs Have To

Both express obligation, but with an important difference.

Must = personal obligation (from the speaker)

I must call my mother. (I feel I should)

Have to = external obligation (from rules/others)

I have to wear a uniform at work. (the company requires it)

Should vs Ought To vs Had Better

Should = general advice: You should eat more vegetables.

Ought to = moral obligation (slightly stronger): You ought to apologise.

Had better = strong warning (consequence implied): You had better leave now or you'll miss the train.

Can vs Could vs May vs Might — Permission

Can = informal permission: Can I sit here?

Could = more polite: Could I sit here?

May = formal permission: May I sit here?

Might = very tentative (rare): Might I ask a question?

Modal Perfects: Past Speculation

Modal + have + past participle expresses speculation or regret about the past.

She must have forgotten — she never misses meetings.

He might have taken a different route.

You should have told me earlier! (regret/criticism)

They could have won if they'd tried harder. (unrealised possibility)

✅ What You Learned

Modal verbs express certainty, obligation, permission and possibility — each with subtle differences in strength and formality. Modal perfects add a past dimension for speculation and regret.
